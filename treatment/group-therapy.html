The Power of Group Therapy

Group therapy is one of the most effective components of addiction treatment. Research consistently shows that peer support significantly improves outcomes—people who participate in group therapy are more likely to stay in treatment, maintain sobriety, and build the support networks essential for long-term recovery.

Addiction thrives in isolation. Group therapy breaks that isolation by connecting you with others who truly understand what you're going through. You'll learn from others' experiences, gain perspective on your own situation, and discover that recovery is possible because you'll see others doing it.

Why Group Therapy Works

  • Universality: Realizing others share similar struggles reduces shame and isolation
  • Hope: Seeing others succeed in recovery provides hope for your own journey
  • Peer Feedback: Honest feedback from peers can be more impactful than from therapists
  • Altruism: Helping others in the group reinforces your own recovery
  • Social Learning: Watch others handle challenges and learn new coping skills
  • Accountability: The group holds you accountable in a supportive way

Types of Groups at Trust SoCal

Process Groups

Open-ended groups focused on exploring feelings, relationships, and patterns of behavior. Process groups help you understand yourself better, work through emotional issues, and practice interpersonal skills in a safe environment.

Psychoeducational Groups

Structured groups that teach specific topics: the science of addiction, relapse prevention, stress management, healthy relationships, and life skills. You'll gain knowledge and practical tools for recovery.

Skills Training Groups

Groups focused on developing specific coping skills. This includes CBT skills groups for changing thought patterns, DBT skills groups for emotional regulation, and mindfulness groups for stress reduction.

Specialty Groups

Groups for specific populations or issues, including:

  • Women's groups - Addressing trauma, relationships, and women-specific issues
  • Men's groups - Addressing anger, emotional expression, and masculinity
  • Trauma groups - For those with significant trauma histories
  • LGBTQ+ groups - Affirming spaces for identity-related discussions
  • Veterans groups - For military service members and veterans
  • Young adult groups - For ages 18-25

12-Step and Alternative Recovery Groups

We facilitate 12-step meetings (AA, NA) and introduce alternative recovery programs (SMART Recovery, Refuge Recovery) so you can find the approach that resonates with you.

What Happens in Group?

A typical group session includes:

  • Check-in: Each member shares how they're doing
  • Topic or Process: Discussion of a topic or exploration of feelings/issues
  • Feedback: Members share observations and support
  • Skills Practice: Trying new techniques (in skills groups)
  • Check-out: Summarizing takeaways and intentions

Group Therapy vs. Individual Therapy

Both individual and group therapy are important parts of comprehensive treatment. They serve different purposes:

Group Therapy Offers:

  • Peer support and connection
  • Multiple perspectives on your situation
  • Practice social and interpersonal skills
  • Learn from others' experiences
  • Accountability to peers
  • Reduced shame through shared experience

Individual Therapy Offers:

  • Private space for sensitive issues
  • Personalized treatment planning
  • Deep exploration of personal history
  • Focused attention on your specific needs
  • Processing of trauma at your own pace
  • Individualized skill development

Overcoming Group Therapy Concerns

"I'm not comfortable sharing in groups"

You're not alone in feeling this way—most people are nervous at first. You're never forced to share, and you can participate at your own pace. Most people find that as they get comfortable with the group, sharing becomes easier and more valuable.

"My problems are too serious/different"

Addiction affects people from all backgrounds, and you'd be surprised how much you have in common with others despite surface differences. The core struggles of addiction—shame, loss of control, damaged relationships—are universal.

"I'm worried about confidentiality"

Confidentiality is taken seriously. All group members commit to keeping what's shared in group private. We discuss confidentiality expectations at the start of every group, and breaking confidentiality has serious consequences.

Group Therapy Guidelines

For groups to be effective, we ask members to follow these guidelines:

  • Confidentiality: What's said in group stays in group
  • Respect: Listen without interrupting, avoid judgment
  • Honesty: Be genuine in your sharing and feedback
  • Participation: Engage actively, even when it's uncomfortable
  • Attendance: Be present and on time—the group depends on you
  • Feedback: Focus on your own experience ("I" statements)
  • Safety: No romantic relationships between group members
