Understanding Cocaine Addiction

Cocaine is a powerful stimulant that floods the brain with dopamine, creating intense euphoria followed by a devastating crash. According to the National Institute on Drug Abuse, cocaine's effects on the brain's reward system make it highly addictive, leading to compulsive use despite serious consequences.

Types of Cocaine We Treat

  • Powder cocaine (hydrochloride): Snorted or dissolved and injected
  • Crack cocaine: Smokable form with faster, more intense effects
  • Speedball: Cocaine combined with heroin (requires specialized detox)

Signs of Cocaine Addiction

  • Using cocaine in larger amounts or longer than intended
  • Inability to cut down despite wanting to
  • Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering from cocaine
  • Intense cravings for cocaine
  • Failing to fulfill work, school, or home obligations
  • Continued use despite relationship problems
  • Giving up important activities due to cocaine use
  • Financial problems from spending on cocaine
  • Needing more cocaine to achieve the same high (tolerance)
  • Experiencing crashes or depression when not using

Fentanyl-Contaminated Cocaine Warning

The CDC reports increasing overdose deaths from cocaine contaminated with fentanyl. If you're using cocaine, you may be unknowingly exposed to this deadly opioid. Testing strips and Narcan can save lives.

Our Cocaine Addiction Treatment Programs

Unlike opioid addiction, there are no FDA-approved medications specifically for cocaine addiction. Treatment relies on proven behavioral therapies that address the psychological nature of stimulant addiction.

Medical Stabilization (3-7 Days)

While cocaine withdrawal is less physically dangerous than alcohol or opioids, medical supervision ensures safety:

  • 24/7 monitoring for cardiac complications
  • Medications for depression, anxiety, and sleep disturbances
  • Nutritional support (cocaine suppresses appetite)
  • Treatment for any co-occurring substances
  • Assessment for dual diagnosis mental health conditions

Evidence-Based Behavioral Therapies

Research shows these approaches are most effective for cocaine addiction:

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Identifies triggers and develops coping strategies
  • Contingency Management: Provides incentives for negative drug tests (proven highly effective)
  • Motivational Interviewing: Enhances internal motivation for change
  • Community Reinforcement Approach: Builds a cocaine-free lifestyle

NIDA research shows Contingency Management is particularly effective for stimulant addiction.

Residential Treatment (30-90 Days)

Our inpatient program removes you from triggers and environments associated with use:

  • Structured daily schedule to replace cocaine-focused routines
  • Individual and group therapy sessions
  • Dual diagnosis treatment for co-occurring depression, anxiety, ADHD
  • Life skills training and healthy coping mechanisms
  • Family therapy and relationship repair
  • Exercise and wellness programs (helps restore dopamine balance)

Outpatient Programs (IOP/PHP)

Flexible treatment while maintaining work or family obligations:

  • Partial Hospitalization (PHP): 5-6 days/week, 6+ hours/day
  • Intensive Outpatient (IOP): 3-5 days/week, 3-4 hours/day
  • Regular drug testing and accountability
  • Ongoing CBT and group therapy
  • 12-step or SMART Recovery integration

Cocaine Withdrawal: What to Expect

Cocaine withdrawal is primarily psychological but can be intensely uncomfortable:

Hours 1-24: The Crash

Extreme fatigue, depression, increased appetite, intense cravings, irritability

Days 1-7: Acute Withdrawal

Depression, fatigue, increased sleep, vivid dreams, continued cravings

Weeks 1-10: Extinction Phase

Mood swings, intermittent cravings triggered by cues, difficulty experiencing pleasure (anhedonia)

Months 1-6+: Post-Acute Withdrawal

Occasional cravings (especially when triggered), gradual return of normal pleasure response

Frequently Asked Questions About Cocaine Treatment

Is cocaine physically addictive?

Cocaine is primarily psychologically addictive, creating intense cravings and compulsive use patterns. While physical withdrawal symptoms are less severe than opioids or alcohol, cocaine causes significant changes to brain chemistry, particularly dopamine systems. The psychological addiction is extremely powerful and requires professional treatment.

What does cocaine withdrawal feel like?

Cocaine withdrawal is primarily psychological rather than physical. Symptoms include intense depression, fatigue, increased appetite, disturbed sleep, vivid unpleasant dreams, agitation, and powerful cravings. The "crash" after stopping cocaine can last days, while depression and cravings may persist for weeks or months.

Are there medications for cocaine addiction?

Currently, there are no FDA-approved medications specifically for cocaine addiction. However, medications may be prescribed to manage withdrawal symptoms like depression and sleep disturbances. Behavioral therapies, particularly Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Contingency Management, are the most effective evidence-based treatments.

How long does cocaine addiction treatment take?

Treatment length varies by individual. Most residential programs last 30-90 days, followed by outpatient care. Because cocaine addiction is highly psychological, longer treatment with strong aftercare support produces better outcomes. Many people benefit from ongoing therapy and support groups for 6-12 months or longer.

Why do people relapse on cocaine?

Cocaine cravings can be triggered by people, places, and things associated with past use. The brain's reward system takes time to heal, and early recovery often involves difficulty experiencing normal pleasure. This is why environmental changes, trigger management, and extended support are crucial for lasting recovery.
