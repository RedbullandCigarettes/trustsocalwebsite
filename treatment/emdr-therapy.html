What is EMDR?

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is an evidence-based psychotherapy that helps people heal from trauma and other distressing life experiences. Originally developed to treat PTSD, EMDR is now recognized as highly effective for addiction treatment, especially when trauma is a contributing factor.

During EMDR, you focus on a traumatic memory while experiencing bilateral stimulation (typically side-to-side eye movements). This process helps your brain reprocess the memory so it no longer triggers intense emotional responsesâ€”reducing the need to self-medicate with substances.

The Trauma-Addiction Connection

Up to 75% of people in addiction treatment have experienced significant trauma. Many people develop addiction as a way to cope with:

  • Childhood abuse or neglect
  • Sexual assault or domestic violence
  • Combat trauma or first responder experiences
  • Accidents, natural disasters, or witnessing violence
  • Loss of a loved one
  • Medical trauma

How EMDR Works

EMDR follows an eight-phase protocol:

  1. History Taking: Identify target memories and treatment goals
  2. Preparation: Learn coping skills and build trust with your therapist
  3. Assessment: Activate the memory and identify related beliefs/emotions
  4. Desensitization: Process the memory with bilateral stimulation
  5. Installation: Strengthen positive beliefs about yourself
  6. Body Scan: Identify and resolve any remaining physical tension
  7. Closure: Return to equilibrium at the end of each session
  8. Reevaluation: Review progress at the start of next session

EMDR Research for Addiction

Studies show EMDR significantly improves addiction treatment outcomes:

  • 77% reduction in PTSD symptoms in addiction patients
  • Lower relapse rates compared to treatment without EMDR
  • Reduced cravings and depression
  • Faster processing time than traditional talk therapy
  • Recognized by WHO, APA, and VA as first-line trauma treatment

What to Expect in EMDR

Unlike traditional talk therapy, EMDR doesn't require you to describe your trauma in detail. You'll:

  • Focus briefly on a disturbing memory or trigger
  • Follow your therapist's fingers (or lights/tones) side to side
  • Notice what thoughts, feelings, or sensations arise
  • Continue until the memory no longer feels distressing
  • Sessions typically last 60-90 minutes

Most people begin to feel relief within 3-6 EMDR sessions, though complex trauma may require more time.
