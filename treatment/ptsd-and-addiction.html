PTSD and Addiction Treatment

Trauma and addiction are deeply connected. If you're using substances to escape the pain of the past, you're not alone—and there is a way to heal from both. Our trauma-informed program treats PTSD and addiction together.

The Connection Between Trauma and Addiction

The link between PTSD and substance abuse is one of the strongest in mental health. According to the National Center for PTSD:

  • Up to 75% of people who survive violent trauma develop problematic alcohol use
  • Veterans with PTSD are 2-4 times more likely to have substance use disorders
  • Women with PTSD are 2.5 times more likely to abuse alcohol and 4.5 times more likely to abuse drugs
  • Among people seeking addiction treatment, 33-50% also have PTSD

Why Do Trauma Survivors Turn to Substances?

PTSD symptoms are overwhelming. Substances offer temporary relief:

To Sleep

Nightmares and hyperarousal make sleep nearly impossible. Alcohol, opioids, or benzodiazepines can knock you out—but they disrupt normal sleep architecture and make PTSD worse.

To Stop Flashbacks

Intrusive memories of trauma can strike without warning. Substances numb these memories and dull their emotional impact—temporarily.

To Calm Hypervigilance

The constant state of high alert is exhausting. Depressants like alcohol slow down the nervous system and provide temporary calm.

To Feel Something—or Nothing

PTSD can cause emotional numbness. Some people use stimulants to feel alive again. Others use opioids to maintain that numbness and avoid pain.

To Function Socially

Trauma often creates isolation and difficulty connecting. Alcohol lowers inhibitions and makes social situations more manageable.

The Vicious Cycle

While substances provide temporary relief, they make PTSD worse over time. Alcohol disrupts sleep and increases nightmares. Drugs prevent your brain from processing trauma. The trauma remains stuck, the symptoms get worse, and you need more substances to cope. Breaking this cycle requires treating both conditions together.

Types of Trauma We Treat

PTSD can result from many types of traumatic experiences. Our clinicians are trained to help with:

Combat and Military Trauma

Combat exposure, military sexual trauma (MST), witnessing death, moral injury, survivor's guilt.

Childhood Trauma

Physical, sexual, or emotional abuse. Neglect. Witnessing domestic violence. Growing up with addicted parents.

Sexual Assault

Rape, sexual abuse, trafficking, intimate partner sexual violence.

Accidents and Disasters

Car accidents, fires, natural disasters, workplace injuries, near-death experiences.

Violence

Assault, robbery, domestic violence, witnessing violent crime, community violence.

Complex Trauma

Repeated, prolonged trauma, often in childhood or captivity situations. May include CPTSD symptoms.

Our Trauma-Informed Treatment Approach

Trust SoCal provides truly integrated treatment where PTSD and addiction are addressed together by the same clinical team, using evidence-based trauma therapies.

EMDR Therapy

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR) is one of the most effective treatments for PTSD. During EMDR:

  • You focus on a traumatic memory while following bilateral stimulation (eye movements, taps, or tones)
  • This helps your brain process the "stuck" memory so it no longer triggers intense reactions
  • Memories become less vivid and distressing
  • Physical stress responses (racing heart, panic) decrease
  • Can also target addiction triggers and cravings

Research shows EMDR can significantly reduce PTSD symptoms in as few as 6-12 sessions.

Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT)

CPT is an evidence-based treatment that helps you examine and change unhelpful beliefs that developed after trauma:

  • "It was my fault" (self-blame)
  • "I can never trust anyone" (safety)
  • "I'm permanently damaged" (self-worth)
  • "The world is completely dangerous" (power/control)

CPT helps you develop more balanced beliefs, reducing PTSD symptoms and the need to self-medicate.

Seeking Safety

Seeking Safety is a therapy specifically designed for co-occurring PTSD and addiction. It focuses on:

  • Building coping skills for both trauma and substance cravings
  • Creating safety in relationships, thoughts, behaviors, and emotions
  • Understanding the connection between trauma and addiction
  • Practical tools for managing triggers without substances

Medication Management

Our psychiatrists can prescribe medications that help with both PTSD and addiction:

  • Prazosin: Reduces nightmares and improves sleep
  • SSRIs: Sertraline (Zoloft) and paroxetine (Paxil) are FDA-approved for PTSD
  • Naltrexone: Reduces alcohol/opioid cravings; some evidence for trauma symptoms
  • Non-addictive anxiety medications: For managing acute distress

Treatment Programs

  • Residential Treatment: 30-90 days of intensive integrated trauma work
  • Partial Hospitalization (PHP): Day treatment with trauma therapy

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is PTSD so closely linked to addiction?

PTSD symptoms are intensely uncomfortable—hypervigilance, intrusive memories, nightmares, emotional numbness. Substances provide temporary escape. Veterans with PTSD are 2-4 times more likely to develop substance use disorders. Unfortunately, self-medication makes PTSD worse over time while creating addiction.

What is trauma-informed addiction treatment?

Trauma-informed care recognizes that most people with addiction have experienced trauma, and that confrontational approaches can retraumatize patients. Our entire program is designed with trauma in mind: physical and emotional safety, choices and control, and evidence-based trauma therapies alongside addiction treatment.

Should I treat PTSD or addiction first?

Research strongly supports treating both together. Treating only addiction without addressing trauma usually leads to relapse—you'll return to substances to cope with unprocessed trauma. Treating only PTSD while actively using is difficult because substances interfere with emotional processing. Integrated treatment is best.

What is EMDR and does it work for addiction?

EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) helps process traumatic memories through bilateral stimulation while recalling distressing events. It's highly effective for PTSD and can also target addiction-related memories and cravings. Many patients experience significant relief within 6-12 sessions.

I'm afraid to talk about my trauma. Will I be forced to?

No. Trauma therapy is collaborative and proceeds at your pace. We'll never force you to share details you're not ready to discuss. Many effective techniques (like EMDR) don't require you to describe your trauma in detail. You're always in control of what you share.

Will working on trauma make me worse before I get better?

Processing trauma can temporarily increase distress—that's normal. However, our therapists are trained to help you stay within your "window of tolerance" and use stabilization techniques when needed. The temporary discomfort leads to lasting relief as your brain finally processes what it's been trying to avoid.
