Understanding Dual Diagnosis

According to SAMHSA's National Survey on Drug Use and Health, approximately 9.5 million adults in the United States have both a mental health disorder and a substance use disorder. This is called dual diagnosis or co-occurring disorders—and it requires specialized integrated treatment.

The Connection Between Mental Health and Addiction

Mental health conditions and addiction are deeply interconnected:

  • Self-medication: People often use drugs or alcohol to cope with mental health symptoms
  • Shared risk factors: Trauma, genetics, and brain chemistry affect both conditions
  • Substance-induced disorders: Drug use can trigger or worsen mental health symptoms
  • Cycle of relapse: Untreated mental health leads to substance use and vice versa

Common Co-Occurring Conditions We Treat

Depression – Persistent sadness, hopelessness, loss of interest, often leads to self-medication with alcohol or opioids
Anxiety Disorders – Generalized anxiety, panic disorder, social anxiety; often co-occurs with alcohol or benzodiazepine addiction
Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) – Trauma-related symptoms; high rates of opioid and alcohol use
Bipolar Disorder – Mood episodes; often co-occurs with stimulant or alcohol addiction
ADHD – Attention and impulse control issues; linked to stimulant misuse and other substance use
Borderline Personality Disorder – Emotional dysregulation; high rates of co-occurring addiction

Our Integrated Dual Diagnosis Treatment Approach

Trust SoCal provides true integrated treatment where addiction and mental health are addressed together by the same clinical team, not in separate silos.

Comprehensive Psychiatric Assessment

Our on-site psychiatrists conduct thorough evaluations:

  • Full psychiatric diagnostic assessment
  • Determination of which symptoms are primary vs. substance-induced
  • Trauma history evaluation
  • Medication review and optimization
  • Cognitive and psychological testing as needed

Psychiatric Medication Management

Non-addictive medications to stabilize mental health:

  • Antidepressants: SSRIs, SNRIs for depression and anxiety
  • Mood stabilizers: For bipolar disorder and emotional regulation
  • Antipsychotics: When indicated for psychosis or severe symptoms
  • Non-addictive anxiolytics: Buspirone, hydroxyzine for anxiety
  • MAT medications: Suboxone, Vivitrol for opioid addiction

We avoid prescribing potentially addictive medications whenever possible.

Evidence-Based Therapies

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Addresses both addiction and mental health
  • Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT): Emotional regulation, distress tolerance
  • EMDR: Trauma processing for PTSD
  • Seeking Safety: Integrated treatment for trauma and addiction
  • Motivational Interviewing: Building motivation for change
  • Group therapy: Peer support with dual diagnosis focus

Treatment Programs

  • Medical Detox: Safe withdrawal with psychiatric support
  • Residential Treatment: 30-90 days of intensive integrated care
  • Partial Hospitalization (PHP): Day treatment with psychiatric services
  • Intensive Outpatient (IOP): Flexible ongoing support

Why Integrated Treatment Matters

Treating Addiction Alone Doesn't Work

If underlying depression, anxiety, or trauma goes untreated, people return to substances to cope with overwhelming symptoms. This is a leading cause of relapse.

Treating Mental Health Alone Doesn't Work

Ongoing substance use undermines psychiatric medications and therapy. The brain can't heal while being bombarded with drugs or alcohol.

Integrated Treatment Works

Research consistently shows that integrated treatment—addressing both conditions simultaneously with a coordinated clinical team—produces significantly better outcomes than sequential or parallel treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions About Dual Diagnosis Treatment

What is dual diagnosis?

Dual diagnosis (also called co-occurring disorders) refers to having both a substance use disorder and a mental health condition at the same time. Examples include depression and alcohol addiction, anxiety and benzodiazepine dependence, or PTSD and opioid addiction. Both conditions must be treated simultaneously for lasting recovery.

Why is integrated treatment important for dual diagnosis?

Mental health conditions and addiction often fuel each other. Treating only addiction without addressing underlying mental health leaves people vulnerable to relapse—they return to substances to cope with untreated symptoms. Integrated treatment addresses both conditions together, significantly improving outcomes.

What mental health conditions commonly co-occur with addiction?

The most common co-occurring conditions include depression, anxiety disorders (including generalized anxiety, social anxiety, and panic disorder), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), bipolar disorder, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, and schizophrenia. Research shows nearly half of people with addiction have a co-occurring mental health condition.

How do you determine if I have a dual diagnosis?

Our psychiatrists conduct comprehensive assessments to distinguish between primary mental health conditions and substance-induced symptoms. Some symptoms may be caused by substance use and will improve with sobriety, while others are independent conditions requiring ongoing treatment. We reassess throughout treatment.

Will I need to take psychiatric medication long-term?

It depends on your specific diagnosis. Some conditions like bipolar disorder typically require long-term medication. Others like substance-induced depression may resolve with sobriety. Our psychiatrists work with you to find the minimal effective treatment, and medication needs are reassessed as you recover.
