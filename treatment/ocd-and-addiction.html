Understanding OCD and Addiction

About 25% of people seeking treatment for OCD also have a substance use disorder. While OCD and addiction are distinct conditions, they share core features—intrusive urges, compulsive behaviors, and difficulty tolerating distress—that can interact in complex ways.

People with OCD often use substances to escape the exhausting cycle of obsessions and compulsions. The temporary relief from intrusive thoughts can feel like a miracle—until substance use creates its own set of problems and ultimately makes OCD symptoms worse.

How OCD and Addiction Interact

  • Self-Medication: Using substances to quiet obsessive thoughts
  • Shared Compulsivity: Similar brain mechanisms drive both conditions
  • Ritual Incorporation: Substance use can become part of OCD rituals
  • Distress Intolerance: Both involve inability to sit with discomfort
  • Worsening Symptoms: Substances ultimately increase anxiety and OCD severity
  • Treatment Interference: Substance use undermines OCD treatment effectiveness

OCD Symptoms and Substance Use

Different OCD presentations may lead to different substance use patterns:

Contamination OCD

Fear of germs or contamination may lead to using alcohol as a "cleanser" or avoiding substances altogether due to contamination fears.

Harm OCD

Intrusive thoughts about harming oneself or others can drive substance use to numb the terror these thoughts cause—or lead to avoidance of substances that might lower inhibitions.

"Just Right" OCD

Need for things to feel "just right" can extend to substance use—needing to use a certain amount, in a certain way, at certain times.

Pure-O (Primarily Obsessional)

Those with primarily mental rituals often use substances to escape the mental exhaustion of constant internal reviewing and analyzing.

Common Substance Patterns

  • Alcohol: Most commonly used to reduce anxiety and quiet obsessive thoughts
  • Benzodiazepines: Sometimes prescribed for OCD-related anxiety, can become addictive
  • Marijuana: Used to "turn off" racing thoughts, though can worsen anxiety
  • Stimulants: Sometimes used to focus on tasks and override OCD interference

Why Integrated Treatment Is Essential

OCD requires specific treatment (Exposure and Response Prevention) that standard addiction treatment doesn't provide. And addiction treatment that doesn't account for OCD may inadvertently worsen symptoms. Integrated dual diagnosis treatment:

  • Provides ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention) for OCD
  • Addresses addiction with evidence-based approaches
  • Recognizes how the conditions interact
  • Coordinates medications appropriately
  • Builds distress tolerance for both conditions

Our Treatment Approach

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)

ERP is the gold standard treatment for OCD. You'll gradually face feared situations or thoughts (exposure) while resisting the urge to perform compulsions (response prevention). This breaks the OCD cycle and teaches your brain that the feared outcomes don't occur.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

CBT addresses both OCD and addiction by helping you identify and challenge the thoughts driving compulsive behaviors—whether those behaviors are rituals or substance use.

Medication Management

SSRIs are first-line medications for OCD and are non-addictive. Our psychiatrists can optimize your medication regimen for both conditions, avoiding potentially addictive medications when possible.

Distress Tolerance Training

Both OCD and addiction involve difficulty sitting with uncomfortable feelings. DBT skills training teaches you to tolerate distress without reaching for compulsions or substances.

Recovery Challenges

Treating co-occurring OCD and addiction presents unique challenges:

  • ERP requires tolerating anxiety without substances
  • Early recovery may temporarily worsen OCD symptoms
  • OCD-related shame can interfere with treatment engagement
  • Relapse on substances often triggers OCD relapse

Our specialized program anticipates these challenges and provides extra support during the difficult early stages.
