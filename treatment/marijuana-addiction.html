Is Marijuana Really Addictive?

Yes. About 9% of people who use marijuana become dependent on it, and that number rises to 17% for those who start in adolescence and 25-50% for daily users. Cannabis Use Disorder is a recognized diagnosis with clear criteria, and today's high-potency marijuana products make addiction more likely than ever.

We understand that many people—maybe even friends and family—don't take marijuana addiction seriously. But if you can't stop despite wanting to, if you're using more than you intend, if your life has narrowed around getting high, then you have a real problem that deserves real help.

Signs of Cannabis Use Disorder

  • Using more: Smoking more often or in larger amounts than intended
  • Failed attempts: Wanting to cut down or stop but being unable to
  • Time spent: Much of your day revolves around marijuana
  • Cravings: Strong urges to use, especially at certain times/situations
  • Neglecting responsibilities: Work, school, relationships suffer
  • Continued use: Using despite problems it causes
  • Withdrawal: Irritability, sleep problems, appetite changes when stopping

The Problem with "It's Just Weed"

Cultural attitudes dismissing marijuana as harmless can make it harder to seek help. You might feel embarrassed or think your problem isn't "serious enough" for treatment. But consider:

  • Today's marijuana is 3-5x stronger than decades ago
  • Concentrates, edibles, and vapes can be even more potent
  • Daily use can lead to significant cognitive impairment
  • Motivation, memory, and emotional development are affected
  • Legal status doesn't determine whether something is addictive

Marijuana Withdrawal

While not medically dangerous, marijuana withdrawal is real and uncomfortable:

Common Withdrawal Symptoms

  • Irritability and mood swings: Often the most challenging symptom
  • Sleep problems: Difficulty falling asleep, vivid dreams
  • Appetite changes: Reduced appetite, weight loss
  • Anxiety: Nervousness, restlessness
  • Physical discomfort: Headaches, sweating, chills
  • Cravings: Strong urges to use, especially in triggering situations

Symptoms typically peak within the first week and improve significantly by week 2-3.

Our Treatment Approach

Non-Judgmental Assessment

We take marijuana addiction seriously without being preachy. Our clinical team assesses your use pattern, mental health, and life circumstances to develop an individualized treatment plan.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

CBT is highly effective for marijuana addiction. You'll learn to identify triggers, challenge thoughts that lead to use, develop coping skills for cravings, and build motivation for change.

Motivational Enhancement Therapy

MET helps you explore your own reasons for change and strengthen your commitment to recovery. This approach is especially helpful when motivation feels ambivalent.

Treatment for Underlying Issues

Many people use marijuana to cope with anxiety, depression, boredom, or trauma. Our dual diagnosis approach addresses these underlying issues so you have healthy ways to manage them.

Life Skills and Structure

Daily marijuana use often replaces other activities. We help you rediscover interests, build routines, develop stress management skills, and create a fulfilling life that doesn't center around getting high.

Treatment Settings

The right level of care depends on your situation:

  • Intensive Outpatient (IOP): Often the best fit—treatment while maintaining work/school
  • Partial Hospitalization (PHP): More intensive day programming
  • Residential: When you need distance from triggers and using environment

What to Expect in Recovery

People who stop daily marijuana use often report:

  • Clearer thinking and better memory
  • More motivation and energy
  • Better sleep (after initial adjustment)
  • Improved mood stability
  • More time for meaningful activities
  • Better relationships
  • Saving money

Young Adults and Marijuana

Marijuana use is especially concerning for young adults (18-25) because the brain is still developing. Heavy use during this period can affect:

  • Memory and learning ability
  • Motivation and achievement
  • Emotional development
  • Risk of developing mental health conditions

Our young adult program addresses the unique challenges facing this age group.
