The Self-Medication Trap

Anxiety disorders are the most common mental health condition in America, affecting 40 million adults. And according to the Anxiety & Depression Association of America, about 20% of people with an anxiety disorder also have a substance use disorder.

Why? Because substances work—at least temporarily. A drink calms the racing thoughts. A Xanax stops a panic attack. Pain pills quiet the constant worry. The problem is that this relief is a trap:

Stage 1: Relief

Alcohol or drugs temporarily reduce anxiety symptoms. The relief feels incredible—finally, your mind is quiet.

Stage 2: Tolerance

Your brain adapts. You need more of the substance to get the same relief. What used to take one drink now takes three.

Stage 3: Dependence

Your brain can no longer regulate anxiety on its own. Without the substance, anxiety spikes to unbearable levels. You're now using just to feel "normal."

Stage 4: Worsening Anxiety

Chronic substance use actually makes anxiety worse over time. Your baseline anxiety is higher than before you started using. You're trapped.

Types of Anxiety Disorders We Treat

  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): Constant, excessive worry about everyday things
  • Panic Disorder: Sudden, intense panic attacks with physical symptoms
  • Social Anxiety Disorder: Intense fear of social situations and judgment
  • Specific Phobias: Irrational fear of specific objects or situations
  • Agoraphobia: Fear of places or situations that might cause panic
  • Health Anxiety: Excessive worry about having a serious illness

Substances Commonly Used to Self-Medicate Anxiety

  • Alcohol: Depresses the central nervous system, temporarily calming anxiety
  • Benzodiazepines: Xanax, Klonopin, Valium—prescribed for anxiety but highly addictive
  • Opioids: Create euphoria and emotional numbing that quiets anxiety
  • Marijuana: Some strains reduce anxiety, but heavy use worsens it
  • Stimulants: Some people use cocaine or meth for confidence in social situations

Special Focus: Benzodiazepine Addiction

If you're taking benzodiazepines (Xanax, Klonopin, Valium, Ativan) for anxiety, you face unique challenges:

Warning: Never Stop Benzos Cold Turkey

Benzodiazepine withdrawal can cause life-threatening seizures. Medical detox is essential. Our medical team will create a safe tapering schedule that minimizes withdrawal symptoms while keeping you safe.

Benzodiazepines are among the most addictive medications prescribed for anxiety. Even when taken as prescribed, physical dependence develops within weeks. Many people who thought they were just "taking their medication" are shocked to discover they're addicted.

The good news: We specialize in benzodiazepine detox and can help you taper safely while transitioning to non-addictive anxiety treatments that work just as well long-term.

Our Integrated Treatment Approach

At Trust SoCal, we treat anxiety and addiction as intertwined conditions that must be addressed together. Our approach combines psychiatric medication management with evidence-based therapies.

Non-Addictive Medication Options

Our psychiatrists specialize in treating anxiety without addictive medications:

  • SSRIs: Prozac, Zoloft, Lexapro—first-line treatment for anxiety disorders
  • SNRIs: Effexor, Cymbalta—effective for GAD and panic disorder
  • Buspirone: Non-addictive medication specifically for anxiety
  • Hydroxyzine: Non-addictive medication for acute anxiety
  • Beta-blockers: Help with physical symptoms like racing heart
  • Gabapentin: Can help anxiety and alcohol withdrawal

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is the gold standard for treating both anxiety and addiction. It teaches you to:

  • Identify catastrophic thinking patterns that fuel anxiety
  • Challenge irrational thoughts with evidence-based reasoning
  • Recognize triggers for both anxiety and substance cravings
  • Develop healthy coping strategies that don't involve substances
  • Build distress tolerance for uncomfortable emotions

Exposure and Response Prevention

A key challenge in recovery: facing anxiety-provoking situations without using substances. Exposure therapy helps you:

  • Gradually face feared situations in a safe, controlled way
  • Learn that anxiety peaks and then naturally decreases
  • Build confidence that you can handle anxiety without substances
  • Reduce overall anxiety sensitivity over time

Mindfulness and Relaxation Training

  • Mindfulness meditation: Observing anxious thoughts without reacting
  • Progressive muscle relaxation: Releasing physical tension
  • Breathing techniques: Activating the parasympathetic nervous system
  • Yoga: Combining physical movement with mindfulness
  • Biofeedback: Learning to control physiological anxiety responses

Treatment Programs

  • Medical Detox: Safe withdrawal, especially critical for benzodiazepines
  • Residential Treatment: 30-90 days of intensive integrated care
  • Partial Hospitalization (PHP): Day treatment with psychiatry
  • Intensive Outpatient (IOP): Ongoing support while living at home

What to Expect in Recovery

Week 1-2: Initial Spike

Expect anxiety to increase as substances leave your system. This is called "rebound anxiety" and it's temporary. Our medical team will help manage symptoms with non-addictive medications while your brain begins to heal.

Week 3-4: Stabilization

As your brain chemistry begins to normalize, anxiety starts to decrease. Therapy helps you develop new coping skills. You'll start to experience moments of calm without substances.

Month 2-3: Building Skills

With continued therapy and possibly medication, anxiety becomes more manageable. You'll practice facing fears without substances, building confidence in your ability to cope.

Long-Term Recovery

Many people find that their anxiety is actually lower in sustained recovery than when they were using. The brain heals, new coping skills become automatic, and life becomes manageable without substances.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do people with anxiety become addicted to drugs or alcohol?

Anxiety is deeply uncomfortable—the racing heart, constant worry, and feeling that something terrible will happen. Alcohol and drugs provide temporary relief. This self-medication works at first, but tolerance develops quickly. Soon you need more to get the same relief, and you've become dependent on substances to function.

Can I take anxiety medication in recovery?

Yes, but the medication type matters. Benzodiazepines (Xanax, Klonopin, Valium) are highly addictive and generally avoided in recovery. However, many effective non-addictive anxiety medications exist: SSRIs, SNRIs, buspirone, hydroxyzine, and others. Our psychiatrists specialize in treating anxiety safely in people with addiction history.

Will my anxiety get worse when I stop using?

Initially, yes. This is called rebound anxiety and it's temporary. With proper treatment including therapy and non-addictive medication, anxiety typically improves significantly within 4-8 weeks. Many people find their anxiety is actually lower in sustained recovery than while using.

What type of therapy helps anxiety and addiction?

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the gold standard for treating both conditions. Exposure therapy teaches you to face anxiety triggers without substances. Mindfulness-based therapies reduce overall anxiety levels. We use all of these approaches in an integrated treatment plan.

I've tried to quit before but the anxiety is unbearable. What's different this time?

Previous attempts may have failed because anxiety wasn't properly treated alongside addiction. Our integrated approach addresses both simultaneously—starting non-addictive anxiety medications during detox, providing intensive therapy, and teaching you skills to manage anxiety without substances. You won't be left to face anxiety alone.
