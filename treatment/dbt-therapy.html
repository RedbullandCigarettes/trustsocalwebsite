What is Dialectical Behavior Therapy?

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) is an evidence-based treatment originally developed for borderline personality disorder, now widely used for addiction treatment. DBT is particularly effective for people who experience intense emotions and use substances to cope.

The word "dialectical" refers to the balance between acceptance and change. DBT teaches you to accept yourself as you are while also working to change harmful behaviorsâ€”including substance use.

The Four Core DBT Skills

  • 1. Mindfulness: Stay present in the moment without judgment. Observe thoughts and feelings without reacting impulsively.
  • 2. Distress Tolerance: Survive crises without making things worse. Learn to tolerate pain without using substances.
  • 3. Emotion Regulation: Understand and manage intense emotions. Reduce vulnerability to emotional triggers.
  • 4. Interpersonal Effectiveness: Ask for what you need, say no, and maintain self-respect in relationships.

Why DBT Works for Addiction

Many people use drugs or alcohol to escape painful emotions. DBT directly addresses this by teaching healthier ways to cope with emotional pain. Research shows DBT:

  • Reduces substance use by 50-70% in studies
  • Decreases self-harm and suicidal behaviors
  • Improves emotional stability and mood
  • Builds healthier relationships
  • Effects last well beyond treatment

Who Benefits Most from DBT?

DBT is especially effective if you:

  • Experience intense, rapidly changing emotions
  • Have a history of co-occurring mental health conditions
  • Struggle with self-harm or suicidal thoughts
  • Have difficulty in relationships
  • Use substances to cope with emotional pain
  • Have borderline personality traits
  • Have tried other treatments without lasting success

DBT vs. CBT: What's the Difference?

While CBT focuses primarily on changing thoughts and behaviors, DBT adds an emphasis on acceptance and emotional regulation. DBT was designed for people with more severe emotional dysregulation. Many treatment programs, including Trust SoCal, use both approaches.
