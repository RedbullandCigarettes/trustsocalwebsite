Understanding Xanax Addiction

Xanax (alprazolam) is one of the most commonly prescribed—and misused—benzodiazepines in the United States. Originally prescribed for anxiety disorders and panic attacks, Xanax produces feelings of calm and relaxation that make it highly addictive. Tolerance develops quickly, meaning you need more to get the same effect, and physical dependence can develop in as little as a few weeks of regular use.

Whether you started taking Xanax with a prescription or obtained it another way, addiction can take hold before you realize it. The fast-acting nature of Xanax makes it particularly prone to misuse, and stopping suddenly after regular use can be medically dangerous.

Warning: Do Not Stop Suddenly

Xanax withdrawal can cause seizures and other life-threatening complications. If you're physically dependent on Xanax, do not attempt to quit cold turkey. Medical detox with a gradual taper is essential for safety. Call us to discuss safe options.

Signs of Xanax Addiction

  • Taking more Xanax than prescribed or for longer than intended
  • Needing higher doses to achieve the same effect (tolerance)
  • Feeling unable to function normally without Xanax
  • Doctor shopping or obtaining Xanax from multiple sources
  • Buying Xanax illegally (from dealers, online, or other people's prescriptions)
  • Withdrawal symptoms when you try to stop or cut down
  • Continuing to use despite relationship, work, or health problems
  • Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering from Xanax
  • Giving up activities you used to enjoy because of Xanax use

Xanax Withdrawal Symptoms

  • Anxiety: Often worse than the original anxiety Xanax was treating
  • Insomnia: Severe difficulty sleeping, sometimes for weeks
  • Panic attacks: Intense, frightening episodes of anxiety
  • Tremors: Shaking, especially in hands
  • Sweating: Excessive perspiration
  • Seizures: Medical emergency requiring immediate attention
  • Perceptual disturbances: Sensitivity to light, sound, and touch

Our Xanax Treatment Approach

Medical Detox with Safe Tapering

Our medical detox program uses a gradual tapering protocol to safely reduce your Xanax dose over time. This prevents dangerous withdrawal symptoms like seizures while minimizing discomfort. Our medical team monitors you 24/7, adjusting your taper as needed.

Cross-Tapering Protocols

For some clients, we use "cross-tapering"—switching from short-acting Xanax to a longer-acting benzodiazepine like diazepam, which is easier to taper. This approach often provides a smoother, more comfortable detox experience.

Treating Underlying Anxiety

Many people start using Xanax because of legitimate anxiety problems. We address the underlying anxiety with therapies like CBT and DBT, and non-addictive medications when appropriate. You shouldn't have to choose between anxiety and addiction—we treat both.

Comprehensive Therapy

Beyond detox, our residential and outpatient programs address the psychological aspects of addiction: why you started using, what triggers your use, and how to build a life where you don't need Xanax to cope.

Xanax Detox Timeline

  • Days 1-3: Stabilization on current or equivalent dose
  • Weeks 1-4: Gradual dose reduction (rate depends on usage history)
  • Weeks 4-8: Final tapering stages, managing rebound symptoms
  • Ongoing: Post-acute withdrawal symptoms may persist for months

Note: Timelines vary significantly based on dose, duration of use, and individual factors.

Post-Acute Withdrawal Syndrome (PAWS)

Even after acute withdrawal, many people experience lingering symptoms for weeks or months. This "post-acute withdrawal syndrome" can include:

  • Ongoing anxiety and mood swings
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Memory problems
  • Increased stress sensitivity

Understanding that these symptoms are temporary and part of recovery helps you push through without relapsing. Our aftercare programs provide ongoing support during this challenging period.

Why Professional Treatment?

Some people try to quit Xanax on their own or through rapid home tapers. This is dangerous because:

  • Seizures can occur without warning
  • The discomfort often leads to relapse
  • Underlying anxiety needs proper treatment
  • Professional monitoring ensures safety
  • Medical interventions can ease symptoms
