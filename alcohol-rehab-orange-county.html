Understanding Alcohol Use Disorder

Understanding Alcohol Addiction

Alcohol use disorder (AUD), commonly called alcoholism, is a medical condition characterized by an impaired ability to stop or control alcohol use despite negative consequences. It exists on a spectrum from mild to severe, with symptoms including:

  • Drinking more or longer than intended
  • Unsuccessful attempts to cut down or stop drinking
  • Spending significant time obtaining, using, or recovering from alcohol
  • Cravings or strong urges to drink
  • Failing to fulfill work, school, or home responsibilities due to drinking
  • Continuing to drink despite relationship problems
  • Giving up important activities because of alcohol use
  • Drinking in dangerous situations (driving, operating machinery)
  • Developing tolerance (needing more alcohol for the same effect)
  • Experiencing withdrawal symptoms when not drinking

If you experience two or more of these symptoms, you may have alcohol use disorder. The severity depends on the number of symptoms: 2-3 symptoms indicates mild AUD, 4-5 indicates moderate AUD, and 6 or more indicates severe AUD.

How Alcohol Affects the Brain

Chronic alcohol use physically changes brain structure and chemistry. Alcohol enhances the effects of GABA (a calming neurotransmitter) while suppressing glutamate (an excitatory neurotransmitter). Over time, the brain adapts to these changes, requiring alcohol to function "normally." This is why withdrawal can be so dangerous—suddenly removing alcohol causes the brain's excitatory systems to become overactive, potentially causing seizures.

The prefrontal cortex, responsible for decision-making and impulse control, is particularly affected by chronic alcohol use. This explains why people with alcohol addiction often struggle to stop drinking despite wanting to—the part of the brain that would normally help them resist cravings is compromised.

Signs You Need Alcohol Rehab

Recognizing when alcohol use has become a problem requiring professional help can be difficult. Here are signs that indicate it's time to consider alcohol rehab:

Physical Warning Signs

  • Needing to drink more to feel the same effects (tolerance)
  • Experiencing shakes, sweating, or nausea when not drinking
  • Morning drinking to relieve withdrawal symptoms
  • Frequent blackouts or memory gaps
  • Health problems related to drinking (liver issues, high blood pressure)
  • Changes in appearance or neglecting personal hygiene

Behavioral Warning Signs

  • Drinking alone or in secret
  • Lying about how much you drink
  • Missing work, school, or family events due to drinking
  • Failed attempts to quit or cut back on your own
  • Drinking despite relationship problems it causes
  • Legal issues related to alcohol (DUI, public intoxication)
  • Continuing to drink despite knowing it's causing problems

Warning: Alcohol Withdrawal Can Be Life-Threatening

Unlike many other substances, alcohol withdrawal can cause fatal complications including seizures and delirium tremens (DTs). Never attempt to quit alcohol "cold turkey" without medical supervision, especially if you've been drinking heavily for an extended period. Professional medical detox ensures your safety during this critical time.

Alcohol Detox: What to Expect

Medical detox is typically the first step in alcohol rehabilitation. During detox, your body clears itself of alcohol while medical professionals manage withdrawal symptoms and prevent dangerous complications.

Alcohol Withdrawal Timeline

6-12 Hours After Last Drink

Early withdrawal symptoms begin: anxiety, headache, nausea, insomnia, sweating, rapid heartbeat. These are usually mild but signal that more severe symptoms may follow.

12-24 Hours

Symptoms intensify. Visual, auditory, or tactile hallucinations may occur. Hand tremors become more pronounced. Blood pressure and heart rate may become elevated.

24-48 Hours

Peak risk period for withdrawal seizures. This is why medical supervision is critical. Medications like benzodiazepines are used to prevent seizure activity.

48-72 Hours

Risk of delirium tremens (DTs) is highest. DTs involve severe confusion, hallucinations, fever, and can be fatal without medical treatment. About 3-5% of people in withdrawal experience DTs.

5-7 Days

Acute withdrawal symptoms typically subside. Some symptoms like sleep disturbances, anxiety, and mood changes may persist for weeks or months (post-acute withdrawal syndrome).

How Medical Detox Keeps You Safe

Professional alcohol detox programs use several strategies to ensure safety and comfort:

  • 24/7 Medical Monitoring: Vital signs, mental status, and withdrawal severity are continuously assessed
  • Benzodiazepine Protocol: Medications like Librium or Ativan prevent seizures and reduce withdrawal severity
  • Hydration and Nutrition: IV fluids and vitamins (especially thiamine/B1) prevent complications
  • Comfort Medications: Antiemetics for nausea, sleep aids, and other medications as needed
  • Emotional Support: Counseling and peer support begin during detox

Alcohol Rehab Treatment Options in Orange County

After completing detox, ongoing treatment addresses the psychological and behavioral aspects of alcohol addiction. Orange County offers a full continuum of care:

Residential (Inpatient) Treatment

Residential treatment provides 24/7 care in a structured environment. This level of care is recommended for:

  • Severe alcohol use disorder
  • History of relapse after outpatient treatment
  • Unstable or triggering home environment
  • Co-occurring mental health conditions
  • Anyone who needs intensive, focused treatment

Residential programs typically last 30-90 days and include individual therapy, group counseling, family therapy, evidence-based therapies (CBT, DBT, EMDR), medication management, and holistic approaches.

Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP)

PHP provides intensive treatment during the day (5-6 hours, 5-7 days/week) while allowing clients to return home or to sober living in the evening. PHP is appropriate for those stepping down from residential treatment or those needing intensive care with a stable living situation.

Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)

IOP offers flexible treatment (3-4 hours, 3-5 days/week) that accommodates work and family responsibilities. Evening and weekend sessions are available. IOP is ideal for those with mild to moderate AUD, strong support systems, or as a step-down from PHP.

Medications Used in Alcohol Rehab

Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT) for alcohol use disorder combines FDA-approved medications with counseling. These medications are safe, effective, and not addictive.

FDA-Approved Medications

  • Naltrexone (Vivitrol): Blocks opioid receptors that alcohol activates, reducing the "rewarding" effects of drinking and cravings. Available as a daily pill or monthly injection.
  • Acamprosate (Campral): Helps restore brain chemistry disrupted by chronic alcohol use, reducing post-acute withdrawal symptoms and supporting abstinence.
  • Disulfiram (Antabuse): Causes unpleasant reactions (nausea, flushing, headache) when alcohol is consumed, creating a deterrent to drinking.

Research shows that combining medication with therapy produces better outcomes than either approach alone. At Trust SoCal, our physicians work with each client to determine if medication is appropriate and which option best fits their needs.

Frequently Asked Questions About Alcohol Rehab

How long does alcohol rehab take?

Alcohol rehab typically involves 5-7 days of medical detox followed by 30-90 days of treatment. The optimal duration depends on addiction severity, co-occurring conditions, and individual progress. Research consistently shows that longer treatment (90+ days total) produces significantly better outcomes.

Is alcohol detox dangerous?

Yes, alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening. Severe withdrawal can cause grand mal seizures and delirium tremens (DTs), which have a mortality rate of up to 37% without treatment. This is why professional medical detox is essential—medications and monitoring prevent these dangerous complications.

Does insurance cover alcohol rehab?

Yes, most insurance plans cover alcohol rehab under the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act. Coverage varies by plan, but many clients pay little to nothing out of pocket. Trust SoCal offers free insurance verification to help you understand your benefits.

What medications are used in alcohol rehab?

FDA-approved medications for AUD include Naltrexone (reduces cravings), Acamprosate (supports abstinence), and Disulfiram (creates deterrent to drinking). During detox, benzodiazepines prevent seizures. Our physicians personalize medication recommendations for each client.

Can I keep working while in alcohol rehab?

It depends on the program. Residential treatment requires taking time off work. However, our Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP) offers evening sessions that allow you to maintain your job while receiving comprehensive treatment.

What's the success rate for alcohol rehab?

Success rates vary based on program quality, treatment duration, and individual factors. Trust SoCal maintains an 85% success rate for clients completing the full program. Key factors include length of treatment, medication use, ongoing therapy, and support group participation.

How do I know if I need residential vs. outpatient?

Residential is recommended for severe AUD, dangerous withdrawal potential, unstable living situation, or co-occurring mental health issues. Outpatient works well for mild-moderate AUD with stable housing and strong support. Our clinical team can help determine the right level of care during your assessment.
