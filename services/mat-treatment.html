Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT)

MAT combines FDA-approved medications with counseling and therapy to treat opioid and alcohol addiction. It's not a crutch—it's evidence-based medicine that can double your chances of successful recovery.

What is Medication-Assisted Treatment?

Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT) uses FDA-approved medications, in combination with counseling and behavioral therapies, to provide a "whole-patient" approach to treating substance use disorders.

According to the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA), MAT is clinically proven to:

  • Reduce opioid use and prevent overdose deaths
  • Increase treatment retention—patients stay in recovery longer
  • Decrease criminal activity associated with addiction
  • Improve employment outcomes and quality of life
  • Lower relapse rates compared to abstinence-only approaches
  • Reduce transmission of HIV and hepatitis C

The Science Behind MAT

Opioid and alcohol addiction change brain chemistry. MAT medications help restore normal brain function by reducing cravings and withdrawal symptoms. This gives your brain time to heal while you learn new coping skills in therapy. It's not "cheating" at recovery—it's using every available tool to save your life.

MAT for Opioid Addiction

For heroin, fentanyl, prescription painkiller, or other opioid addiction, three FDA-approved medications are available:

Buprenorphine (Suboxone, Sublocade)

How it works: Buprenorphine is a partial opioid agonist—it activates opioid receptors just enough to prevent withdrawal and reduce cravings, but not enough to produce a high. Suboxone combines buprenorphine with naloxone to deter misuse.

  • Suboxone: Daily dissolving film or tablet
  • Sublocade: Monthly injection (no daily dosing)

Benefits: Can be prescribed by certified physicians in office settings. Significantly reduces relapse, overdose, and death. Can be used long-term or tapered gradually.

Naltrexone (Vivitrol)

How it works: Naltrexone is an opioid antagonist—it completely blocks opioid receptors. If you use opioids while on naltrexone, you won't feel any effect. This removes the incentive to use.

  • Oral naltrexone: Daily pill
  • Vivitrol: Monthly injection (more effective due to adherence)

Benefits: Non-addictive with no diversion risk. No withdrawal symptoms if stopped. Good option for those who want complete abstinence from all opioids.

Requirement: Must be fully detoxed from opioids (7-14 days) before starting.

Methadone

How it works: Methadone is a full opioid agonist that satisfies opioid receptors without producing euphoria when taken as prescribed. It has a long duration of action, preventing withdrawal for 24-36 hours.

Availability: Due to regulations, methadone for addiction treatment can only be dispensed through certified opioid treatment programs (OTPs). Trust SoCal can provide referrals to methadone programs if this is the best option for your situation.

MAT for Alcohol Addiction

Three FDA-approved medications can help treat alcohol use disorder:

Naltrexone (Oral or Vivitrol)

How it works: Naltrexone reduces the rewarding effects of alcohol by blocking endorphin release. Drinking becomes less pleasurable, reducing the desire to drink.

  • Reduces heavy drinking days
  • Decreases cravings
  • Can be used with a "targeted" approach (taking before social situations)

Acamprosate (Campral)

How it works: Acamprosate helps restore the brain's chemical balance disrupted by chronic alcohol use. It reduces post-acute withdrawal symptoms like anxiety, insomnia, and restlessness.

  • Best started after detox is complete
  • Helps maintain abstinence
  • Particularly helpful for those with severe, long-term alcohol dependence

Disulfiram (Antabuse)

How it works: Disulfiram causes extremely unpleasant reactions if you drink alcohol—flushing, nausea, vomiting, rapid heartbeat. The threat of this reaction deters drinking.

  • Works as a deterrent, not a craving reducer
  • Most effective for highly motivated individuals
  • Requires complete abstinence (reactions can occur 2 weeks after stopping)

Debunking MAT Myths

Myth: "MAT is just trading one addiction for another."

Truth: MAT medications don't produce a high when taken as prescribed. They stabilize brain chemistry without the dangerous cycles of intoxication and withdrawal. Being physically dependent on a medication that keeps you alive is not the same as addiction. Diabetics depend on insulin—we don't call them "addicted" to it.

Myth: "You're not really sober if you're on MAT."

Truth: Recovery is about reclaiming your life—not about suffering through withdrawal. MAT patients can work, maintain relationships, and participate fully in therapy. They have better outcomes than those who try abstinence-only approaches. The definition of "sober" is evolving as we understand more about addiction as a medical condition.

Myth: "MAT is the easy way out."

Truth: Recovery is hard work regardless of whether you use MAT. You still need therapy, lifestyle changes, and commitment. MAT simply reduces the overwhelming cravings and withdrawal that make early recovery so difficult—giving you a better chance of success.

Myth: "You should get off MAT as soon as possible."

Truth: There's no rush. Research shows that longer treatment with MAT leads to better outcomes. Premature discontinuation often leads to relapse. The goal is sustainable recovery—however long that takes and whatever tools help achieve it.

Our MAT Program

At Trust SoCal, MAT is integrated into our comprehensive treatment approach:

Medical Assessment

Our addiction medicine physicians evaluate your substance use history, medical conditions, and preferences to recommend the most appropriate medication. Not everyone needs MAT, and not every medication works for every person.

Induction & Stabilization

If MAT is appropriate, we carefully initiate medication—either during detox or after. For opioid medications, this induction process requires careful monitoring to find the right dose.

Ongoing Monitoring

Throughout treatment, our medical team monitors your response to medication, adjusts dosages as needed, and addresses any side effects. Regular check-ins ensure the medication continues to support your recovery.

Integrated Therapy

Medication alone isn't treatment—it's part of treatment. Our program includes individual therapy, group counseling, family therapy, and life skills training. MAT supports this work by reducing cravings so you can engage fully.

Aftercare Planning

Before discharge, we coordinate ongoing MAT prescriptions with outpatient providers. Whether you continue MAT or eventually taper, we ensure continuity of care.

Available at All Levels of Care

MAT can be incorporated into medical detox, residential treatment, partial hospitalization, and intensive outpatient programs. We meet you where you are.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is MAT just trading one addiction for another?

No. MAT medications don't produce a high when taken as prescribed. They stabilize brain chemistry, reduce cravings, and prevent withdrawal—allowing you to focus on recovery. Research shows MAT patients have better outcomes and lower relapse rates.

How long will I need to be on MAT?

There's no one-size-fits-all answer. Some people use MAT short-term, others benefit from longer maintenance. The decision is collaborative with your medical team based on your needs and recovery goals. What matters is staying in recovery—and MAT significantly increases those odds.

What's the difference between Suboxone and Vivitrol?

Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone) is a daily medication that reduces cravings and prevents withdrawal. It's a partial opioid agonist. Vivitrol (naltrexone) is a monthly injection that blocks opioid receptors entirely, preventing any high if you use. Both are effective; the choice depends on your situation.

Does MAT work for alcohol addiction?

Yes. Naltrexone reduces alcohol cravings and the rewarding effects of drinking. Acamprosate helps reduce post-acute withdrawal symptoms. Disulfiram causes unpleasant reactions if you drink. These medications significantly improve outcomes when combined with therapy.

Will my insurance cover MAT?

Most insurance plans cover MAT medications, especially since the Mental Health Parity Act. Our admissions team can verify your specific coverage and help you understand any costs. We're in-network with Health Net, and work with most major insurance providers.

Can I participate in 12-step programs while on MAT?

Absolutely. While some traditional 12-step groups may have outdated views on MAT, many are now "MAT-friendly." Organizations like Medication-Assisted Recovery Anonymous (MARA) specifically welcome people on medications. Your recovery is valid regardless of how you achieve it.
