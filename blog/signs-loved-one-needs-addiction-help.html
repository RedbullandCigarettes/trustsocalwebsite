10 Signs Your Loved One May Need Addiction Treatment

Recognizing the warning signs of addiction and knowing when it's time to seek help.

By Trust SoCal Clinical Team | January 5, 2026 | 8 min read

Watching someone you love struggle with substance abuse is one of the most painful experiences a family can face. You might notice changes in their behavior, personality, or appearance—but wonder if you're overreacting. Is this just a phase? Are they really addicted? When is it time to intervene?

If you're asking these questions, trust your instincts. You know your loved one better than anyone, and if something feels wrong, it probably is. Here are 10 warning signs that may indicate your loved one needs professional addiction treatment.

1. They've Lost Control Over Their Use

The hallmark of addiction is loss of control. Someone who can "take it or leave it" doesn't have an addiction. Someone who cannot stop despite wanting to, who uses more than they intended, or who has tried to quit multiple times without success—they're showing signs of addiction.

What to look for:

  • They say they'll only have "one drink" but consistently have many more
  • They've made promises to cut back but haven't followed through
  • They become anxious or irritable when they can't use
  • Their intentions to stop are sincere, but they keep relapsing

2. They Need More to Get the Same Effect

Tolerance is a major warning sign. When someone first starts using a substance, a small amount produces a significant effect. Over time, the brain adapts, and they need increasingly larger doses to achieve the same feeling. This isn't a choice—it's biology.

What to look for:

  • What used to be "a few drinks" has become a bottle
  • Their pain medication dosage keeps increasing
  • They use in the morning to "feel normal"
  • They seem unfazed by amounts that would affect most people

3. They Experience Withdrawal Symptoms

When someone is physically dependent on a substance, stopping causes withdrawal symptoms—and these can range from uncomfortable to life-threatening. If your loved one feels sick, anxious, or agitated without the substance, their body has become dependent.

What to look for:

  • Shakiness, sweating, or tremors
  • Nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea
  • Insomnia or disturbed sleep
  • Anxiety, irritability, or mood swings
  • Flu-like symptoms without being sick
Important: Withdrawal from alcohol and benzodiazepines can cause seizures and be life-threatening. If your loved one wants to stop using these substances, they need medical detox—never attempt to quit "cold turkey."

4. Their Personality Has Changed

Addiction changes who people are. The person you knew may seem like a stranger—more secretive, more irritable, less interested in things they used to love. Substances hijack the brain's reward system, and over time, getting and using the substance becomes more important than anything else.

What to look for:

  • They've become secretive or lie frequently
  • They're more irritable, defensive, or angry
  • They've lost interest in hobbies and activities
  • Their values and priorities have shifted
  • You feel like you're "walking on eggshells" around them

5. They're Neglecting Responsibilities

As addiction progresses, it takes over more and more of a person's life. Responsibilities that once mattered—work, school, family, bills—fall by the wayside. The substance becomes the priority.

What to look for:

  • Missing work or school frequently
  • Declining performance at job or studies
  • Neglecting household duties or childcare
  • Unpaid bills or financial problems
  • Forgetting important commitments

6. Relationship Problems Are Mounting

Addiction destroys relationships. Your loved one may be more isolated, have conflicts with family and friends, or have entirely new social circles (often people who enable their use). You may feel like you're losing them.

What to look for:

  • Increased conflict with family members
  • Loss of long-time friendships
  • New friends who also use substances
  • Withdrawal from social activities
  • Broken trust due to lies or broken promises

7. Their Physical Appearance Has Declined

Addiction takes a physical toll. Poor nutrition, dehydration, sleep disruption, and the effects of the substances themselves can cause noticeable changes in appearance.

What to look for:

  • Significant weight loss or gain
  • Poor hygiene and grooming
  • Bloodshot eyes, dilated or constricted pupils
  • Skin changes (pallor, sores, track marks)
  • Looking older or more tired than they should

8. They're Experiencing Financial Problems

Addiction is expensive. Substances cost money, and as tolerance builds, the costs increase. Many people with addiction develop financial problems—even those who previously managed money well.

What to look for:

  • Unexplained money problems
  • Borrowing or stealing money
  • Selling possessions
  • Unpaid bills despite adequate income
  • Secretiveness about finances

9. They Continue Despite Consequences

One of the clearest signs of addiction is continued use despite obvious negative consequences. A DUI doesn't stop them from drinking. A warning from their boss doesn't stop them from showing up impaired. Destroyed relationships don't stop them from using. This isn't a lack of willpower—addiction has rewired their brain.

What to look for:

  • Legal problems related to substance use
  • Health problems that continue to worsen
  • Job loss without behavior change
  • Relationship endings that don't motivate sobriety
  • Near-death experiences (overdoses) followed by continued use

10. They've Become Isolated and Secretive

People with addiction often isolate themselves—partly from shame, partly to hide their use, and partly because substance use has become their primary relationship. If your loved one has become withdrawn, defensive, and secretive, addiction may be the reason.

What to look for:

  • Spending more time alone
  • Avoiding family gatherings or events
  • Being evasive about whereabouts
  • Getting defensive when asked about their activities
  • Hiding substances or paraphernalia

What Should You Do?

If you recognize several of these signs in your loved one, it's time to take action. Here's how to help:

1. Educate Yourself

Understanding addiction as a medical condition—not a moral failing—will help you approach your loved one with compassion rather than judgment. The more you understand about their specific substance, the better equipped you'll be to help.

2. Choose the Right Time to Talk

Don't try to have a serious conversation when they're intoxicated or when emotions are running high. Find a calm, private moment when you're both sober and relatively stress-free.

3. Use "I" Statements

Instead of "You're ruining your life," try "I'm scared because I love you and I've noticed changes." Express concern, not accusations. Lead with love, not anger.

4. Listen Without Judgment

They may open up about struggles you didn't know about. Let them talk. Don't interrupt with advice or criticism. Sometimes being heard is what they need most.

5. Set Boundaries

Love doesn't mean enabling. Be clear about what behavior you will and won't accept. And follow through—boundaries only work if they're enforced.

6. Consider Professional Help

A professional interventionist can guide your family through a structured conversation that's more likely to result in your loved one accepting treatment. Our admissions team can connect you with intervention resources.

7. Take Care of Yourself

You can't pour from an empty cup. Family programs, Al-Anon, and therapy for yourself are not selfish—they're necessary. You need support too.

When to Call Immediately

If your loved one is experiencing any of the following, don't wait—get help now:

  • Signs of overdose (unconsciousness, slow breathing, blue lips)
  • Suicidal statements or behavior
  • Seizures or severe withdrawal symptoms
  • Violence toward self or others

Call 911 or go to the nearest emergency room.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between substance use and addiction?

Substance use becomes addiction when the person loses control. Can they stop when they want to? Do they need more for the same effect (tolerance)? Do they have withdrawal symptoms? Has use affected relationships, work, or health? If yes, it's likely addiction.

Should I confront my loved one about their addiction?

A loving conversation is usually better than confrontation. Choose a time when they're sober. Use "I" statements. Listen without judgment. Don't expect immediate acceptance—denial is common. Consider professional intervention if conversations don't work.

What if they refuse to get help?

You can't force recovery. But you can: set boundaries, stop enabling, consider professional intervention, and take care of yourself. Sometimes people need to experience consequences before they're ready. That doesn't mean you've failed.
